Q.67 Is it always possible to normalize a wavefunction by multiplying it by an appropriate constant?

Normalization Condition:

To normalize a wavefunction, it should be multiplied by an appropriate constant (other than zero) such that the sum of probability to find the particle over entire space is 1:

(1) ∫ ∞-∞ | Ψ (x) |² dx = 1, where | Ψ(x)|² = ( Ψ*(x)) ( Ψ(x))

A² = 1 ⁄B

A = 1⁄√B

So, assuming that the integral ∫1 Ψ(x)|² dx = B can be calculated,

Ψ(x) is normalized if it is multiplied by 1⁄√B

However it is not always possible to normalize a wavefunction by multiplying it by an appropriate constant.

For instance, a plane wave function

Ψ(x,t) = >Ψ e i(kx-wt)

The integral of its square over all space yields infinity. In quantum mechanics, normalization of a wavefunction requires the integral to be equal to 1. There is no any complex number that can be used to multiply infinity to make it equal to one.

Thus a wavefunction can only be normalized if the wavefunction is single-valued, square-integrable, and continuous.
